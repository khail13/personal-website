EV Battery Mobility Device

Purpose

Through my experiences with the electric vehicle industry, I have become aware of a formidable challenge that engineers, mechanics, and technicians face. Electric vehicle battery modules are heavy, large, and fragile. Transporting these modules within a workshop or factory is a strenuous and daunting task and it often requires the manual labour of two or more people to lift and maneuver these devices. This process is inefficient and can be dangerous. To combat this challenge, I took upon the task of designing a device that could reduce safety risks and increase efficiency within workplace settings while transporting EV batteries.

Design

The three primary goals of this design were as follows…

  • Enable the movement of battery modules to variable heights with a mechanical system to improve workplace efficiency of transporting such large and heavy modules.
  • Reduce the effort required to accomplish the strenuous task by implementing a mechanical lifting system that is capable of being powered electrically to minimize the safety risks of heavy lifting.
  • Lessen operation requirements of the lift to require only a singular person for the maneuvering and lifting of an EV battery. This will reduce labour costs for companies and allow for faster replacement and installation of battery modules.

Through a comprehensive and iterative design process consisting of multiple phases, numerous analyses and modifications were made. The process began with a thorough research phase. The focus of this phase was to investigate the real-world metrics and properties of EV batteries. This included research into aspects such as average weights and maximum sizes of modules and packs alike. The discovery of possible methods of raising such modules were found here. The resulting conclusion was to design a cost-effective scissor lift cart with ease of manufacturability in mind.

To accomplish this, a mechanism was modeled and analyzed using readily available commercial parts and materials including, caster wheels, L-beams, sleeve bearings, strut trolleys and channels, hydraulic pistons, and clevis pins. The resulting assembly met the initial goals and metrics set at the start of the design process and the goal of improving workplace efficiency without sacrificing safety had been achieved. Attaining the desired minimum and maximum heights (0.5m and 1.75m respectively) while maintaining an acceptable safety factor in all loaded components was crucial to the success of the design. The components of the system with the greatest loads, the pins and scissor arms, met the desired safety factor of 4, which was verified using FEA analyses in SOLIDWORKS. By achieving these metrics, the design is an effective and versatile tool to aid in the transportation of EV batteries.

The design can be improved to make the manufacturing process of the product more feasible. The large safety factors contribute immensely to the cost as they can lead to “over-designing” and hence more material required than necessary. The decision to implement large safety factors in the preliminary calculations were to combat many simplifications and assumptions (i.e. disregarding the z-direction in the static calculations and thus neglecting torsion) which left many uncertainties when transitioning from statics to dynamics. More detailed calculations would allow for reduction in the required safety factor in the range of 2 to 2.5 which would reduce the required pin diameters and section moduli for the scissor members and thus decrease the weight and cost of the system. Additional features can also be added in the future to extend the product's effectiveness such as a tipping-bed which would aid the loading process.
